AI Means AskIndia

Why India Should Lead the World in AI Deployment, Not Development

Paa Sankar June 2026 5 min read

A recent headline screamed that India's GenZ is "40 years behind" the world on AI. The metric used: percentage of workforce trained in AI skills. South Korea at 96%. India at 2.3%. Civilisational failure, apparently.

Wrong diagnosis. Wrong metric. Wrong race.

Neelkanth Mishra, newly appointed World Bank Executive Director, said it plainly last week: "We are going to do extremely well when the narrative shifts from generation of intelligence to deployment of intelligence." He is right — but the argument goes further than even he articulates.

India does not need to build the next large language model. Anthropic's CEO has publicly noted that the next version of Claude will be substantially written by Claude itself. The foundational model race will be won or lost by three or four massively capitalised American and Chinese labs. No Indian startup can or should enter that arena. The leverage point is one layer above: what do you build with the tools once they exist?

India has done this before. We did not invent the radio frequency — we built the world's cheapest data economy on it. We did not discover the drug molecules — we became the world's pharmacy through frugal manufacturing. ISRO did not invent rocketry — it became the global reference for lean space programmes by innovating relentlessly on cost.

Cars are perhaps the most instructive analogy. India does not need to know automotive engineering to buy and drive a car that solves its commute problem. But to sell in India, any car must satisfy the Indian notion of value — or it remains a premium product selling in hundreds rather than hundreds of thousands. That market pressure, sustained at scale, is what shaped Maruti into a genuinely world-class manufacturer. Indian-designed cars, forged in the crucible of Indian cost-consciousness and road conditions, are today exportable to markets that are equally cost-sensitive. AI will follow the same arc. Global AI companies will customise for India not out of charity but out of market necessity. And that customisation pressure, applied at a billion-user scale, will produce AI solutions robust enough to travel to every similarly complex, similarly cost-conscious economy in the world.

What makes India's position structurally unique is not our IIT pipeline or our startup ecosystem — though both matter. It is our public data infrastructure. Aadhaar, UPI, DigiYatra, GSTN, NDHM — this is a dataset corpus no foreign lab can replicate or purchase. Training AI on Indian faces, Indian voices, Indian financial behaviour, Indian health patterns — that is a sovereign moat. And critically, most Indians will benefit from AI without ever knowing it is there — the way NEFT transformed payments without users understanding settlement systems, or the way Green Revolution seeds transformed agriculture without farmers understanding Mendelian genetics. The end consumer need not know prompt engineering or diffusion models any more than a Maruti driver needs to understand torque curves.

The deployment opportunity is already underway. Non-banking finance companies are underwriting credit for 300 million credit-invisible Indians using alternative data — utility payments, GST filings, mobile behaviour — that no Western credit model was ever designed to read. Subsidy targeting and Public Distribution System leakage detection are being transformed at scale. Vernacular health delivery is reaching across 22 languages. These are not pilots or proofs of concept. They are live deployments affecting real lives, built on Indian data, solving Indian problems, without a single end-user needing to understand what a large language model is.

The policy architecture must match this ambition. NITI Aayog and the IndiaAI Mission should pivot from the temptation to announce India's own ChatGPT — a headline win that solves nothing — to mandating DPDP-compliant, locally hosted, Indian-dataset-trained AI for all public-facing deployments. Startup support should be structured by problem domain — health, credit, agriculture, vernacular education, welfare delivery — not by technology vertical. The question to fund should not be "are you building a better model?" but "are you solving a problem that matters to a hundred million Indians?"

There is also a military lesson worth absorbing. Cheap drones and use-and-throw kamikaze munitions have fundamentally disrupted the calculus of million-dollar missiles and billion-dollar defence systems. Asymmetric, problem-fitted, numerically overwhelming solutions have outperformed prestige technology at every turn. India's AI strategy should carry the same logic. Not the most powerful model — but the most useful one. Not the highest benchmark score — but the highest lives-improved per rupee of deployment cost.

The deepest argument, however, is philosophical. The global AI race as currently framed is materialistic competition — who has the most parameters, the most compute, the most market capitalisation. India's civilisational contribution has always posed a different question: not who wins, but who is served. We gave the world the understanding that the relentless pursuit of material superiority is not a worthy organising principle for human life. We can now demonstrate that the same principle applies to technology.

Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam — the world is one family — is not a slogan. It is a design principle.

If AI works for an ASHA worker in rural Bihar, for a smallholder farmer managing monsoon unpredictability in Vidarbha, for a first-generation borrower in a Tier-3 town in Tamil Nadu — it will work for a subsistence farmer in sub-Saharan Africa, for an informal worker in Indonesia, for an unbanked household in Nigeria. India is not building AI solutions for India alone. India is stress-testing solutions for humanity, in the most complex, linguistically diverse, economically heterogeneous proving ground on the planet. If it works here, it works everywhere.

We are not late to the AI race. We are early to the right question.

AI does not mean Artificial Intelligence for India. It means AskIndia — because the answers we find here are the answers the world needs.

— Paa Sankar
