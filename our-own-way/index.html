We Have Our Own Way of Living. Did We Forget?

On measurement, civilisational worth, and the mosquito coil

Paa Sankar June 2026 4 min read

Someone showed me an article recently. Impressive statistics. India has the highest number of STEM graduates. India spends only 0.65% of GDP on R&D. USA spends 3.45%. China 2.65%.

The conclusion? India needs to be more like them.

I am a bit sick of it.

We are always comparing Indian statistics with global statistics in everything. And we are always looking to ape what others are doing. Some part of the world already does what we do — but we never seem to show pride in that.

Here is what nobody is saying out loud.

  • A tortoise mosquito coil — our staple, in every Indian home, made by grandmothers, burned on summer nights — is now being repackaged in France as scented spirals and sold for hundreds of euros.
  • Turmeric latte — haldi doodh that every Indian grandmother made for free — is $8 in Manhattan.
  • Yoga retreats charging $3,000 a week teaching what village India practices daily.
  • Ashwagandha sold at 50× markup in US health stores as a miracle adaptogen.

The mosquito coil did not change. The knowledge did not change. Only the currency changed. And just the currency arbitrage gives the West enormous clout in the global economy.

Think about this for a moment.

One company in the US can become bigger than our complete listed market. One person in the West can have more wealth than our GDP.

Does this make sense?

Only if you accept their ruler as the right one to measure with.

That billionaire's wealth exists largely as a story — future cash flows, capitalised at today's prices, denominated in the world's reserve currency. A currency whose dominance was decided not by merit but by who held the gold in 1944 at Bretton Woods.

We export real goods. They export paper. And we call this a fair measure of progress.

But here is what I keep coming back to.

None of this is going to change what we need to do as a nation to solve our own problems. India's problems need India-scale solutions. Clean water for 1.4 billion people is not a problem Silicon Valley needs to solve. Healthcare at ₹500 a visit cannot be designed by a $200-an-hour consultant. Building cities for 50 million rural migrants a year has no Western precedent to copy from.

And when we stopped looking for external validation — we actually did extraordinary things.

  • Aadhaar. The largest biometric identity system in human history. Built at a fraction of what Western consultants estimated.
  • UPI. A payments revolution the US still hasn't replicated.
  • ISRO's Mars mission. Done for less than the budget of a Hollywood movie.
  • Mid-day meals for more schoolchildren than any nation has ever fed.

None of these happened by benchmarking against America.

We gave the world zero. The decimal system. Algebra. A grammar so precise that Panini in 500 BC prefigured modern computer language theory. Yoga. Philosophy. Ayurveda. Sustainable living practices that the world is now rediscovering and repackaging and patenting and selling back to us.

And we are measuring ourselves against an R&D spending percentage?

The article that started all this said: India has the highest STEM graduates. Then said: India doesn't spend enough on R&D. Then never connected the two. Because there is no connection to make. It is two impressive-sounding statistics placed next to each other to make you feel inadequate. To make you think the solution is to become more like someone else.

That is not analysis. That is a sales pitch.

India at its best has always been a synthesiser. We absorb, adapt, and contribute back. We don't need to reject the world's knowledge. We just need to stop evaluating ourselves through a lens that was never calibrated for us.

The mosquito coil was always worth hundreds of euros. We just never thought to charge for it.
