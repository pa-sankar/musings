History Repeats in the Future — Or Will It?

An historical village reimagined in the near future

Paa Sankar June 2026 16 min read
I

A Morning in the Village — Past and Future

Past

The sun has barely cleared the eastern treeline when Murugan steps into his field. The air still carries the cool memory of night. Around him, the paddy stands tall — two weeks from harvest. On the western edge of his plot, a row of drumstick trees marks the boundary with his neighbour Selvam's land, where brinjal and green chilli grow in orderly mounds.

Murugan does not grow only paddy. No farmer in this village does. Each family tends a small kitchen garden alongside their main crop — greens, gourds, tubers, whatever the season allows. The village as a whole grows everything it needs. Not because anyone planned it that way. Simply because hunger is a good planner.

By mid-morning, the village has already fed itself. The temple kitchen has been lit since before dawn. Volunteers — today it is the oil-presser's wife and two women from the weaver street — have cooked a simple meal of rice, sambar, and greens. Anyone who is hungry comes and eats. The farmer whose crop failed last season. The old widow with no sons. The wandering Siddha physician who arrived last evening with his bundle of dried herbs. All eat the same food from the same vessel. No questions asked. No names recorded.

This is not charity. This is the village breathing.

Future

Five kilometres from a highway, a different Murugan steps into his field at the same hour. The paddy looks the same. The morning air feels the same. But something has changed at the edge of the village. Where the old community hall once stood, there is now a low building with solar panels on its roof — the village canteen. Inside, two cooks are already at work, the induction stove humming, the smell of sambar drifting into the morning air.

The canteen is run by the panchayat. It is not a temple kitchen — it belongs to no religion and excludes no one. The orphan children who sleep in the rooms at the back are already awake, helping to set out vessels. The three elders who live here — whose families moved to cities long ago — sit in the courtyard catching the early sun.

Anyone from the village can walk in for breakfast, lunch, or dinner. They pay a nominal amount — enough to signal that they are customers, not recipients. Enough to preserve dignity without creating a barrier. The food is cooked from what the village grows. The gas that fuels the stove comes from the biogas plant fed by last night's kitchen waste and the gobar from the two cows tethered nearby.

The village is breathing again. In the same rhythm it always has. Just with different instruments.

II

How the Surplus Moved — Past and Future

Past

By afternoon, Murugan has loaded a small cart. Not with all his produce — most of it is already spoken for within the village. The potter's family gets drumstick in exchange for pots. The blacksmith's household receives greens every week in exchange for the iron tools they sharpen each season. These transactions are older than money. They do not need a market.

What remains after the village has fed itself — this is what goes on the cart.

A trader named Raman comes from the nearby town every week. He knows what the village has in surplus. He pays a fair price — because he has no alternative. There is no other farmer selling the same brinjal this week within his reach. He loads what Murugan and three other farmers offer him and carries it to the town. In the town, Raman sells to a larger merchant who consolidates produce from five or six villages and sends it to the district town. The district merchant sells to the city trader who moves goods between regions.

Each step adds distance and therefore price. But — and this is the crucial part — each step is dealing in genuine surplus. The village kept what it needed. The town took what the village could spare. The city received what the town could forward. Nobody extracted survival from anyone else.

The city was, in this world, a consumer of surplus. Not a consumer of necessity.

Future

The modern Murugan has a contract with the village canteen. Before he sells a single kilo of paddy to the government procurement agency, the canteen gets first right of purchase — at a fair, pre-agreed price, set at the start of each season. The canteen's cold storage room, powered by solar panels, holds what it needs for the months ahead. The excess alone goes to the procurement agency or the town trader.

This one reversal — village eats first, market gets the rest — changes every power relationship in the chain. The trader can no longer threaten to walk away because Murugan's paddy is already spoken for. The city cannot manufacture artificial scarcity because the village is not desperate. And Murugan, knowing the canteen will buy his produce at an agreed price regardless of what the market does that week, can plan his crop without fear.

The 1% surcharge — collected by the panchayat on every kilo of produce that leaves the village boundary — flows quietly into a repayment fund. The government loan that built the solar panels, the cold storage, the biogas plant, and the water distribution system is being repaid, season by season, from the prosperity the infrastructure itself created. No grants. No dependency. A loan that repays itself.

III

What Specialisation Did — And What It Cost

Past

Someone in a distant office noticed that Murugan's village grew excellent brinjal. And that a city two hundred kilometres away had buyers willing to pay three times what the local town paid. The logic was simple: grow more brinjal, ship it to the city, earn more money.

Murugan listened. He uprooted his drumstick trees. He stopped growing greens. He converted his kitchen garden into more brinjal plots. His neighbour Selvam did the same. The whole village did the same, because the same trader came to all of them with the same logic.

The village that once grew everything it needed now grew only one thing.

For a few seasons, it worked. The city paid well. Murugan bought rice from a shop — grown in some distant delta he would never see. He bought greens that came in a truck — grown by a farmer in another state who had, by the same logic, stopped growing brinjal.

Then one season, the rains were good across every brinjal-growing region simultaneously. Supply exploded. The city trader, now with ten sources instead of one, played them against each other. The price Murugan received collapsed to almost nothing.

The village had surplus brinjal it could not sell. And no rice. And no greens. And no drumstick. And no kitchen garden. And the temple kitchen, which once cooked from what the village grew, now had nothing to cook because nothing grew here except unsellable brinjal.

The young left first. Not for art or education or adventure — though they told themselves those stories. They left because survival now required cash, and cash required a city. The city had factories and construction sites and shops. The city could convert labour into money. And the village could not.

Future

The modern version of this story is told every year in agricultural distress data. A tomato farmer in Maharashtra who received ₹1 per kilo at the farm gate while Chennai consumers paid ₹80 per kilo in the same week. The supply chain ate the difference. Maharashtra specialised in tomatoes. Tamil Nadu stopped growing them. Both became hostage to a chain that neither controlled.

COVID stopped the trucks for three weeks. Cities that had outsourced their food supply to distant specialised farms discovered how thin the margin between convenience and starvation actually was.

The answer is not to ban specialisation. It is to sequence it correctly. The village grows what it needs first — a diverse kitchen garden, a rotation of crops, paddy and pulses and vegetables in the same season. The surplus of whatever it is best at growing then flows outward. Specialisation operates on the margin, not on the survival core.

The panchayat's automatic crop insurance — renewed every season without the farmer needing to apply, negotiate, or remember — removes the catastrophic downside that forces distress selling. When the harvest fails, the insurance compensation flows within weeks, not years. The farmer does not need to sell the next season's crop in advance to pay this season's debt. The debt spiral that has ended so many farming lives simply does not begin.

IV

The Village That Governed Itself — Past and Future

Past

In 920 CE, in a village called Uttiramerur in what is now Tamil Nadu, the village assembly inscribed its governance rules on the walls of the local temple. In stone. For everyone to read.

The inscription specified exactly who could stand for election to the village council. Candidates had to own land and a house within the village. They had to be of a certain age — experienced enough to govern, young enough to serve actively. They had to be literate. They had to have clean accounts — no irregularity in handling village funds, no family member who had previously defaulted on village responsibilities.

The disqualifications were specific and severe. Anyone who had served on the council before and been found wanting was barred from standing again. Anyone who had handled common funds and failed to account for them was barred — and so was any close relative. Corruption did not just disqualify the individual. It disqualified the family.

Terms were limited. Rotation was mandatory. The selection was done by lot from among the qualified candidates — removing the possibility of a permanent ruling clique.

This was not a theoretical constitution. It was operational governance, inscribed in a durable medium, enforced by a community that lived with its consequences. It worked for generations.

Future

One thousand one hundred years later, we are still struggling to get gram panchayat governance right.

The solution is already inscribed on a temple wall in Tamil Nadu. We simply need to read it and update it for today.

The modern adaptation: candidates must be resident in the village — not merely registered there but actually living there, drinking the same water, breathing the same air, eating in the same canteen. A person governing the village water supply must depend on that water supply.

Criminal proceedings against any member of a candidate's immediate family are automatic disqualification — not because families are collectively guilty, but because in Indian village social reality, family is the unit of power and therefore the unit of accountability.

Two terms maximum for any individual. After two terms, sit out at least one full cycle. No panchayat dynasty. No permanent chairman who has held the position since before the youngest voter was born.

Three-year terms — long enough to implement a solar installation or negotiate a crop insurance contract, short enough to correct a mistake before it becomes permanent.

The elected person governs what they live with. The school they fund is where their grandchildren study. The canteen they manage is where they eat lunch. The road they build is the one they walk home on at night. This alignment of interest between governor and governed is not a nice-to-have. It is the foundational mechanism of accountable governance. Uttiramerur understood this. We seem to have forgotten.

V

Economic Self-Sufficiency — Past and Future

Past

No village existed in isolation from the larger political economy around it. Even the most self-sufficient village had seasons of drought, floods that destroyed the harvest, invasions that disrupted trade, or simply the accumulated weight of a bad decade. When that happened, the village turned to the king.

But the king's intervention in those times was not a handout. It was an investment with a long memory.

A king who wished to be remembered — and they all did — came to the village and built. A temple, first, because the temple was the village's civic centre, its granary, its records office, its community hall, and its identity all in one stone structure. With the temple came an endowment: land donated to the temple trust, the revenue from which would feed the temple kitchen in perpetuity. Cattle donated so the kitchen would always have milk and the fields would always have draught animals. A tank dug or deepened so the fields would have water regardless of the monsoon's mood.

The king did not give money. He gave productive assets — land, water, animals, infrastructure — that generated their own revenue in perpetuity. The village used that revenue to maintain itself, feed its hungry, and send its surplus outward. From that surplus, the king's treasury eventually received its share in taxes.

It was not charity. It was the oldest form of patient capital — an investment in productive capacity that repaid itself many times over across generations.

The village of Uttiramerur itself survived and thrived across centuries of political change precisely because successive rulers understood this logic. The inscriptions on its temple walls are not just governance documents. They are evidence of a village whose institutions were strong enough to attract royal investment and capable enough to put that investment to work.

Future

The government of a self-governing democracy is the king's functional successor. It holds the power to mobilise capital, build infrastructure, and create the conditions for productive communities. What it has largely forgotten is the king's instinct for investment over handout.

The financing model for the reimagined village is simple and self-closing.

The government — through NABARD, rural development funds, or direct panchayat lending — extends a structured loan to each village panchayat that commits to a self-sufficiency plan. The loan funds the physical infrastructure of self-sufficiency: solar panels for homes and the canteen, a biogas plant fed by kitchen and farm waste, a central water tank with piped distribution, cold storage for produce, a canteen building that also houses the orphanage and elder care, and a telecom tower shared with the village.

This is not a grant. It is a loan with a specific repayment mechanism built into the village economy itself.

Every kilo of produce, every litre of processed goods, every handicraft or manufactured item that leaves the village boundary through formal trade carries a 1% self-sufficiency surcharge. The panchayat collects this at the cold storage and procurement point — the natural chokepoint through which village surplus passes. The surcharge flows into a dedicated repayment account. When the loan is repaid, the surcharge does not disappear. It becomes the village's permanent development fund — for the next solar array, the school extension, the road repair, the next generation of infrastructure.

The surcharge is self-calibrating. When the village produces more surplus — because the infrastructure made it more productive — repayment accelerates. Prosperity speeds up the exit from debt. The government, like the king before it, eventually receives its return not just in loan repayment but in the taxes generated by a thriving, self-sufficient community that no longer requires perpetual subsidy.

Alongside the infrastructure loan sits the second pillar of economic self-sufficiency: automatic crop insurance. Every farmer in the village is insured every season. Not because they applied. Not because an agent visited. Not because they remembered to renew before the deadline. Because the panchayat, as the collective representative of the village economy, maintains a blanket insurance policy that covers every registered cultivator automatically.

The panchayat has every incentive to maintain this insurance without lapse — because an uninsured crop failure does not just destroy one farmer's livelihood. It destroys the canteen's supply contract, depletes the cold storage, and threatens the village's entire food security architecture. The insurance is not a welfare benefit to the individual farmer. It is risk management for the village as a system.

When the harvest fails — and some years it will — the compensation flows in weeks, not years. No protracted claim process. No field inspection that arrives six months after the damage. The farmer does not need to sell next season's crop in advance to repay this season's debt. The moneylender's grip — which has ended more farming lives than any drought — simply finds no purchase. The debt spiral does not begin because the floor holds.

The king built a temple and donated land. The government builds solar panels and guarantees crop insurance. The logic is identical. The village breathes, produces, and repays. The circle closes.

VI

The Young Who Left — And Why They Will Return

Past

When the village could no longer feed itself, it could no longer hold its people. The young left for the city. They sent money home. They visited during festivals. Over time, the visits became shorter and the money became the only connection. The village became a place to be from, not a place to be.

The city received them. Put them to work in construction, in factories, in domestic service, in the vast informal economy that keeps urban life running. Paid them wages that seemed large against village income but left nothing after city rent, city food, and city transportation. Kept them in a permanent state of being almost-but-not-quite able to save enough to go home.

This is the migration trap. The city needs the migrant's labour to function. The migrant needs the city's wages to survive. Neither can afford to let the other go.

Future

The young person from a self-sufficient village leaves for a different reason and on different terms.

They go to the city for education. For the exposure that a connected village cannot yet provide — the experience of working in a large organisation, of navigating a complex economy, of building professional skills and networks. This is the city's legitimate value and always has been.

They build a career. Start a business, perhaps. Develop a skill that can be delivered from anywhere — design, code, accounting, consulting, content, legal drafting, financial analysis. The digital economy does not care where you sit when you deliver it.

And then they do the calculation that city professionals across the world are increasingly doing: a ₹1.5 lakh per month remote income in Chennai requires ₹1.2 lakh to live on. The same income in their village of origin requires ₹40,000. The difference — ₹80,000 per month — is real surplus. Not theoretical savings. Actual money that compounds.

The village they return to is not the one their parents left. It has solar power, reliable water, a canteen where their children can eat clean food, a 5G tower that keeps them as connected as any city office. Their children grow up knowing where food comes from, speaking both the language of the village and the language of the global economy. They have the best of both circles.

And the village gains something that no government scheme can provide: a returning citizen who brings capital, skills, networks, and the most powerful signal possible to the next generation considering whether to stay or leave.

The migration that remains — and there will always be some — is chosen, not forced. That is the entire difference.

VII

The Infrastructure of Universal Basic Survival

Past

The village survival floor required no infrastructure beyond what the community already provided for itself. The temple kitchen fed the hungry. The community well watered the village. The common land pastured the animals. The seasonal exchange of labour handled the harvest. The wandering physician handled the sick.

None of this was designed. It evolved because a community that allows its members to die of preventable causes is a community that has failed its most basic function. Every human settlement that has persisted across generations has developed some version of this floor — because settlements that didn't, didn't persist.

Future

We can build this floor deliberately. With better materials, better technology, and — thanks to Uttiramerur and a thousand other examples — better governance than our ancestors had access to.

Energy: Solar panels on every household roof. The village is energy sovereign for daily living. Farms stay connected to the grid for the heavy lifting — irrigation pumps, processing equipment. The canteen runs on biogas from its own waste stream, with solar cooking as primary backup and induction as the last resort when clouds and low waste coincide. The village does not wait for the power ministry to decide its priority.

Water: A central tank fed by the local well, pumped by solar power, distributed by pipe to every household and to the canteen. Clean water is not a privilege in this village. It is infrastructure, like the road.

Food: The canteen is the heart of the system. Secular, panchayat-run, open to all. Orphans and elders live here — their care is embedded in an institution that is already cooking, already functioning, already staffed. The marginal cost of caring for twelve elders in a kitchen that serves two hundred people is almost nothing. The canteen buys local produce first — paddy from Murugan before the procurement agency sees it, vegetables from the kitchen gardens before the town trader arrives. What it cannot source locally, it stores in the cold room from seasonal purchases. Nothing is wasted. Everything is circular.

Health: A doctor visits once a week, anchored to the orphanage clinic that exists within the canteen complex. The clinic serves the whole village at the same visit. Telemedicine handles what the weekly visit cannot — the 5G tower that the telecom company placed just outside the village, shared between panchayat administration and all residents, makes this possible at negligible marginal cost.

Incentive alignment: The pesticide question solves itself. When the canteen that serves the whole village — including the farmer's own family — buys the farm's produce, the farmer has a direct personal reason to grow clean food. What they cannot eat, they do not grow for others. No certification body. No organic inspector. No regulatory enforcement. Just the oldest food safety system in human history: the person who grows your food also eats it.

VIII

The Happiness Index

The government of Bhutan made a decision in 1972 that the rest of the world has spent fifty years slowly realising was correct: Gross National Happiness is a more honest measure of how a society is doing than Gross Domestic Product.

GDP measures the volume of transactions. It counts the cigarettes sold to the lung cancer patient and the chemotherapy that follows as equal contributions to national prosperity. It counts the cost of the traffic jam — the fuel burned, the productivity lost, the stress accumulated — as economic activity. It does not count the meal a mother cooks for her family, the story a grandfather tells his grandchildren, the hour a farmer spends watching his field in the early morning quiet.

The village described in this article would score poorly on some GDP metrics. It imports less. It transacts less with the formal economy. The canteen meal that costs ₹30 does not generate the GST, the supply chain margin, the logistics revenue, and the platform fee that a Swiggy delivery generates.

But the child who grows up in this village — who eats clean food, drinks clean water, knows every elder in the community, has a field to run in and a sky with stars in it and a grandmother who is still present rather than in a distant city facility — that child's wellbeing is not captured in any GDP calculation.

The Universal Happiness Index measures what actually matters: community trust, time with family, sense of purpose, relationship with the natural world, cultural continuity, physical and mental health, freedom from existential anxiety about survival.

A village that has solved its survival floor scores well on all of these by default. Not because someone designed happiness into it. Because when survival is not a daily emergency, human beings naturally turn toward the things that make life worth living.

We do not need Universal Basic Income. We need Universal Basic Survival — and then we need to measure whether people are actually happy, rather than merely counting how much they spent trying to get there.

IX

The Nomad Returns

And then, once a year, they come.

A troupe of performers arrives at dusk. Word spreads through the village before they have parked their vehicle. The children come running first, then the adults, phones going into pockets as they step out of the canteen and the fields.

For three nights, the village square becomes a stage. Stories are told that the village has never heard. A musician introduces a composition that the village musicians will spend the next year learning. A visiting doctor shares a new protocol for managing the rice paddy's soil microbiome. News arrives of ideas and movements from distant places — not through an algorithm that has decided what the village should see, but through a human being who has travelled and chosen what to carry.

This nomad is not a relic. They are a consultant, an artist, a scientist, a trader in rare seeds, a documentary filmmaker, a philosopher. They come because the village is interesting — because a community that has solved its survival and is genuinely thriving is more interesting than a city that is frantically generating transactions.

They leave after three nights. The village watches them go with a mixture of satisfaction and longing. The longing keeps the village curious and alive. The satisfaction keeps it rooted and strong.

These are the correct emotions for a community that has remembered what it always knew.

The Same Story, Told Twice

The village that fed itself in the tenth century and the village that can feed itself in the twenty-first century are not separated by a thousand years of progress. They are separated by two hundred years of forgetting.

We forgot that food is grown by people who eat it. We forgot that a community that cannot feed its own members is not a community but a labour pool. We forgot that governance works when the governed and the governor share a water supply, a road, a canteen, a destiny.

Uttiramerur remembered for us. It wrote the answer in stone so we would have no excuse.

The question has never been whether this is possible. Villages across South Asia, across Africa, across Latin America, have been demonstrating for centuries that it is not only possible but natural — the default state of human settlement before specialisation, extraction, and distance disrupted the circle.

The question is only whether we have the imagination to look at the inscription on the temple wall, understand what it means for today, and begin.

The sun rises. Murugan steps into his field. The canteen begins to serve breakfast.

The story has always been the same story.

A village that was, comes back to the future.

This article is an invitation to think differently about what development means, what governance is for, and what we owe each other as members of the same circle. The ideas are old. The technology is new. The human need they serve is eternal.

A note on Uttiramerur

The village of Uttiramerur in Kanchipuram district, Tamil Nadu, contains stone inscriptions from approximately 919–921 CE detailing the rules of village self-governance under the reign of Parantaka I. These inscriptions — among the earliest democratic governance documents in the world — specify candidate qualifications, disqualification criteria, term limits, and selection procedures for village council members. They are publicly accessible and have been studied by historians, political scientists, and governance researchers. They are not a myth. They are a specification document. One that still works.
