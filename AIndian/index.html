It Takes an Indian to Understand an AIndian

Same facts. Fewer straight faces.

Paa Sankar June 2026 ~20 min read

Somewhere in California, a very expensive neural network looked at a retinal scan and figured out it could predict a heart attack. Nobody taught it to look there. It just… noticed. Which is either the greatest party trick in the history of medicine, or proof that AI is the cousin who shows up to the wedding, makes one shockingly accurate observation about your love life within five minutes, and you’re not sure whether to be impressed or to ask them to leave.

That’s the problem with AI in one sentence: it sees patterns, but it doesn’t know anything. It’s an outsider with excellent eyesight and zero context. And nowhere does that show up funnier — or more expensively — than when you ask a model trained on the rest of the world to understand India.

Call it an AIndian. Built everywhere else. Deployed here. Confused immediately.

Let’s tour the crime scene in two parts: Digital India, where the AIndian gets lost in our apps, and Physical India, where it gets lost in our actual bodies.

I

Digital India — Where the AIndian Gets Mugged, Politely

Finance: UPI, Aunty, and the Case of the Suspicious ₹50

Every month, Indians fire off roughly 21.7 billion UPI transactions. That is not a typo, and it is also not a number any rule-based fraud system signed up for. The old system used to work like a bouncer with a clipboard — flag anything over a certain amount, flag anything from an odd location, done. Fraudsters figured the clipboard out fast. So banks switched to AI, trained directly on UPI’s own transaction history — and here’s the thing worth pausing on: there was never a “global UPI” to borrow a model from in the first place. UPI doesn’t exist anywhere else on Earth in this form. So for once, the AIndian wasn’t an immigrant who showed up confused — it was raised here from birth, because there was nowhere else for it to be raised. The good news: it works well enough that the RBI now insists every blocked transaction comes with an explanation, because “the algorithm had a vibe” is not an acceptable answer to a furious customer whose salary just got frozen.

The funnier problem is credit scoring. AI is now trying to decide who deserves a loan by reading their UPI history like tea leaves — and there’s an estimated $130–170 billion reason to get this right, since roughly 350 million Indians have no formal credit history at all. But here’s the joke nobody’s laughing at yet: a model could easily decide that a certain spending rhythm “smells like risk,” when what it’s actually detected is somebody’s caste, geography, or the fact that they live somewhere the bank just doesn’t like very much. An AIndian doesn’t know the difference between “financially risky” and “lives in a pincode the model has prejudices about.” Only a human who actually understands Indian credit markets can tell the two apart — preferably before the loan gets rejected, not after.

Also Finance: The Fake Invoice Olympics

If UPI fraud is petty crime, GST fraud is the national team sport nobody admits to watching. India’s Input Tax Credit system accidentally invented a whole new genre of fraud: register a shell company, issue invoices for goods that never moved an inch, claim a tax credit on a transaction that only ever existed on paper. Repeat at industrial scale. Over five years, this added up to roughly ₹7.08 lakh crore in detected evasion — and fake-ITC cases alone more than doubled in just two years, from ₹24,140 crore to ₹58,772 crore. That’s not a leak in the system. That’s a parallel economy with its own invoices, its own shell companies, and apparently its own five-year plan.

Here’s the bit that makes it genuinely India-specific: catching this means cross-referencing five separate government databases that don’t naturally talk to each other — GST filings, company registration, income tax, banking records, export data — a job that used to mean an actual human being logging into five different portals and squinting. The first fix was just big-data matching: does this invoice’s goods movement match the e-way bill? Simple, useful, and immediately outgrown, because shell company networks aren’t a “weird number” problem, they’re a “who’s secretly invoicing whom in a circle” problem — which is exactly the kind of question graph analytics was built for, and plain spreadsheet thinking wasn’t. The fraud has gotten clever enough that authorities are now eyeing agentic AI just to keep pace, because the moment officials publicise how they caught the last shell company, the next batch of shell companies politely restructures around it. It’s less a crime wave than a long-running rematch.

Traffic: Horn OK Please

Researchers studying AI traffic models have a polite term for what happens when you drop a foreign-trained model onto Indian roads: “spatial generalisation failure.” The rest of us would call it “the model has never seen a bus, an auto, a cow, and a wedding procession occupy the same 40 feet of road at the same time, and it has given up.”

Here’s the best part. Tesla — a company that has more real-world driving data than possibly any organisation in human history — looked at Indian roads and did not say “we’ve got this.” They said “give us four years.” Tesla currently sells Full Self-Driving in India as a non-refundable ₹6 lakh add-on that cannot be switched on. The cars can park themselves. That’s it. Meanwhile India sits on Tesla’s own rollout tracker under “pending approval,” politely waiting its turn behind New Zealand. If the company with the most miles on Earth needed a four-year dedicated rebuild before attempting an Indian junction, maybe stop assuming your chatbot-adjacent traffic app figured it out over a long weekend.

Weather: The Model Said Drizzle. Chennai Disagreed.

Global AI weather models — GraphCast, Pangu-Weather, the greatest hits — are genuinely excellent at predicting weather, provided the weather in question has the decency to behave like weather elsewhere. Hand them the South Asian monsoon and they get… enthusiastic, but slightly unreliable, like a tourist confidently giving you directions in a city they’ve never visited. Researchers actually tested this by checking the models against real ground stations instead of the satellite data the models usually get graded on, and found the errors were 15–45% bigger than the models’ own report cards suggested.

The model had, essentially, been marking its own homework and giving itself an A.

India’s fix: stop importing the homework. The Bharat Forecasting System is being built and trained specifically on Indian terrain and monsoon behaviour, because cloudbursts in Coimbatore don’t read the same textbook as snowstorms in Colorado.

II

Physical India — Where the AIndian Meets Our Actual Bodies and Panics

Food: AI Thinks Payasam Is Rice Pudding. Your Patti Disagrees.

Most food-recognition AI was trained on a world of cuisine that treats “Indian food” as a single, vague rumour. Researchers had to build entirely separate Indian food datasets because the existing models genuinely could not tell moong dal from chana dal — visually near-identical, nutritionally not even in the same conversation.

Now hand this AI a bowl of payasam and watch it have a small breakdown. Payasam isn’t one dish, it’s a mood — jaggery or sugar, coconut milk or dairy, rice or vermicelli or moong dal, and a ghee-to-festivity ratio that scales with how big the occasion is. A model that’s only ever seen “rice pudding” will look at two bowls of payasam that are nutritionally worlds apart and confidently tell you they’re the same thing. The fix the researchers landed on, essentially, was: stop trusting the AI alone, get a human who’s actually eaten the dish to check its work. Which is just a very academic way of saying “ask someone’s grandmother.”

Body Proportions: One Size Fits All, Except Your Wife!

India is currently rolling out a brand-new national shoe sizing standard, ‘Bha,’ for the single most embarrassing reason possible: nobody ever measured Indian feet before deciding what size we should wear. A 3D scan of over 100,000 people confirmed what your slightly-too-tight sandals already told you — Indian feet are, on average, wider than the European and American feet the global sizing charts were built around. We’ve been wearing the wrong-shaped shoes as a nation for decades because someone in a different country, decades ago, measured a different foot.

The S/M/L on your T-shirt has the exact same backstory and nobody’s fixed it yet. Hand an AI styling app a photo of an Indian body and ask it to recommend a size, and it’s making the same mistake the shoe industry just spent a hundred thousand foot-scans correcting.

Health Parameters: Normal on the Chart, But Your Doctor Knows Better!

This is the one that should genuinely worry you. A study of over 444,000 people found South Asians carry the highest cardiovascular risk of any group studied — at the same blood pressure reading. To get the same protection a White patient gets at 140 mmHg, a South Asian needs to be treated as high-risk at around 123 mmHg. Same goes for BMI: a White patient’s diabetes risk threshold sits around 30; a South Asian hits the same risk at roughly 24. There’s even a real medical term — “thin-fat phenotype” — for Indians who look perfectly fine on a weighing scale while quietly stockpiling dangerous fat around their internal organs like a squirrel preparing for a winter that’s already arrived.

Now put an AI health app on top of that. It’s not just misreading the patient — it’s been handed the wrong answer key from the start, because the “normal” range it was trained to trust was never measured on an Indian body in the first place. This isn’t the AI’s fault, exactly. It’s like blaming a tourist for using a map that someone drew of the wrong city.

III

Scale India — Where Even the Measuring Tape Gives Up

Quick vocabulary check before we get into this one. When an Indian says “lakh” or “crore,” most of the world quietly has no idea what number just happened. A lakh is 100,000. A crore is 10 million. We didn’t invent new words because “thousand, million, billion” felt boring — we invented them because at some point those units stopped being granular enough for how often we needed to talk about really large numbers of people, rupees, and meals. In India, scale isn’t a metaphor. It’s a unit of measurement. We measure in different scales because the rest of the world’s scales ran out of zeroes.

Akshaya Patra: The Kitchen That Outgrew the Kitchen

Somewhere in Bangalore, an organisation that started by cooking lunch for 1,500 schoolchildren in the year 2000 now cooks up to 2 million meals a day. Not per year. Per day. The kitchens that emerged from this are not big versions of a home kitchen — they’re a different invention entirely, with rice cauldrons that hold 500 litres and dal cauldrons that hold up to 3,000, running on gravity instead of staff, because at some point you stop hiring more cooks and start hiring physics. One kitchen alone can produce 100,000 meals and have them on a truck, on a rural route, in a child’s hands by lunchtime. Nobody else on Earth had a reason to engineer this. It’s not that other countries couldn’t. It’s that nobody else’s “casual Tuesday” required feeding a number of children that most nations would call a small city.

Roti Machines: When Breakfast Becomes an Engineering Discipline

Somewhere else, quietly, a much funnier kind of scale problem got solved: how do you mechanically replicate the specific, slightly magical thing that happens when a thin disc of dough hits a hot surface and puffs up into a roti. Turns out this is genuinely hard — it’s not the same problem as a bread machine, because nobody needs their loaf to inflate like a tiny balloon on cue. India now manufactures roti machines that can turn out anywhere from 300 to 2,000 rotis an hour, built for hostels, canteens, gurudwaras, and industrial kitchens that needed this exact, oddly specific miracle performed thousands of times a day without a human standing over a tawa. And because nobody else’s daily bread does this party trick, nobody else built the machine first. Now it’s exported to more than fifteen countries, sold to people who didn’t know they needed a roti robot until an Indian factory showed up with one.

Cricket Streaming: When “Casual” Means 65 Million People Hitting Refresh at Once

Somewhere in the world, a sports broadcaster plans capacity for a “big match” and calls it a day. In India, “big match” turned out to be a moving target that keeps demolishing whatever the previous global record was, on a roughly annual basis, whether anyone asked it to or not. An India-Pakistan game alone has, at various points, drawn 35 million concurrent streaming viewers, then 53 million for a World Cup semi-final, then 59 million for the final — and by March 2026, an India-England T20 World Cup semi-final hit 65.2 million peak concurrent viewers on JioHotstar, officially the highest concurrency ever recorded for any live event on any digital platform on Earth, with 619 million total views making it the most-streamed T20 International match in history. That’s not India’s biggest match. That’s a semi-final. Against England, not even the designated rival.

OTT platforms operating anywhere else on the planet build infrastructure for their worst-case Black Friday. Indian streaming platforms build infrastructure for “what if 65 million people decide, simultaneously, to refresh the stream the moment Kohli walks out to bat.” Every single record above was the previous record holder’s “this should never happen” scenario, made completely routine by the next tournament. Surviving in Indian streaming isn’t a product strategy. It’s a structural engineering problem wearing a subscription model.

Infrastructure: We Can Pave a Highway Faster Than We Can Load a Results Page

In January 2026, the National Highways Authority of India laid a continuous, three-lane-wide stretch of bituminous concrete 52 kilometres long — 156 lane-kilometres, using 57,500 metric tonnes of material — in a single 24-hour period, and walked away with a Guinness World Record for it. Days earlier, the same agency had already broken two other Guinness records on the same highway corridor. At this point it’s less a milestone and more a recurring Tuesday for Indian road crews.

Indian Railways, meanwhile, moves more people every single day than the entire population of Australia — roughly 23 million daily riders, on a network first laid down under British rule, somehow still running upwards of 14,000 trains a day without the whole thing visibly falling over. Move a country’s worth of humans, daily, casually, and apparently nobody even blinks anymore.

Then there’s Tatkal, which deserves its own paragraph because it’s the rare case where the scale problem and the fraud problem arrived together and had to be solved at the same time. Roughly 2.5 lakh Tatkal tickets get booked every single day, with nearly 80% of seats on the hundred-odd highest-demand trains snapped up within the first fifteen minutes of the booking window opening — which is exactly the kind of artificial scarcity that invites gaming. And it got gamed, badly: during the worst months, the Railway Ministry found that bots accounted for up to half of all login attempts in the first five minutes of Tatkal opening, and in October 2025 alone, 17 billion of the 24 billion total requests hitting the system were automated — touts and agents running scripts that filled in passenger details faster than any human could click. The fix, again, was Aadhaar: linking Tatkal bookings to Aadhaar-authenticated accounts, banning agents from booking in the first window, and blocking over 3 crore suspicious accounts. It’s working, mostly — IRCTC hit a record 31,814 tickets booked in a single minute on May 22, 2025, proof the platform itself can take the load — but the spike hasn’t gone away, it’s just gone legitimate. Black-market Telegram groups now sell pre-verified Aadhaar-linked IRCTC IDs for around ₹360 each, because where there’s scale, there’s apparently always someone trying to sell a shortcut through it. Same daily scramble. Same millions of people hitting “book” at the exact same second. Just, for the first time, mostly winning.

And yet. Somehow, the same nation that just streamed cricket to 65 million concurrent devices without breaking a sweat cannot reliably survive the CBSE Class 12 results going live. Every single year, without fail, the official results website crashes the instant scores are announced, because a few lakh seventeen-year-olds hit refresh at the exact same second to find out if they got into the engineering college of their dreams. This isn’t a one-off. It’s so reliably annual that “alternative ways to check your CBSE result when the site crashes” is now a permanent, evergreen genre of journalism, republished fresh every May like clockwork. We can lay 52 kilometres of national highway in a day. We have, demonstrably, not yet worked out how to serve one static webpage to half a million anxious teenagers at the same moment.

That’s the honest footnote to “Scale India”: some of our scale problems get solved with bulldozers and Guinness World Records, or with the kind of streaming infrastructure built to survive a cricket final. Others get solved with “try refreshing again in ten minutes” and a prayer to whichever deity is on duty that week. Same country, same genuinely staggering appetite for handling enormous numbers — just wildly different track records depending on whether the bottleneck is asphalt, a server farm built by a company with a market cap, or a government webpage built by the lowest bidder.

Elections: We Built a Machine So We Wouldn’t Have to Cut Down a Forest Every Five Years

India ran elections on paper ballots until the late 1990s, which is a sentence that sounds unremarkable until you remember the number of ballots a country of this size needs printed, folded, sealed, transported, and counted by hand, every single time. The Election Commission’s own estimate puts the saving at around 10,000 tonnes of ballot paper and roughly 20 lakh trees per national election once Electronic Voting Machines took over — battery-operated, deployable in places without reliable electricity, simple enough to use with a single button press. India didn’t build EVMs because it was feeling especially eco-conscious. It built them because the alternative was deforestation as a recurring electoral expense. And true to the pattern by now, the invention didn’t stay home — Indian-made EVMs have since been used in elections in Bhutan, Nepal, Namibia, and Kenya, the same “built for our scale, exported once the rest of the world caught up” story we’ve now told about roti machines and UPI.

Candidates: When One Constituency Outnumbers a National Parliament

Here’s a number that should sound impossible: in 1996, the Belgaum Lok Sabha constituency in Karnataka had 456 candidates contest a single seat — 452 of them independents, almost all from one local political grouping, all fighting over one parliamentary chair. Iceland’s entire national parliament has 63 seats. Malta’s has 65. That one Indian constituency, on its own, in one election, fielded more candidates than several entire countries’ governments combined. The Election Commission was sufficiently alarmed by this that it raised candidate deposit amounts specifically to stop it from happening again — and it mostly worked, except that by 2019, the Nizamabad constituency in Telangana still managed 185 candidates for one seat, which is still comfortably more than the total membership of multiple national parliaments. We don’t just have a lot of voters. We apparently have enough people willing to run for office that a single Indian constituency can, on a bad year, outnumber a small country’s entire government.

Hollywood Box Office: When India Decides If You’re a “Global Hit”

Hollywood studios used to treat the Indian release as a courtesy stop — dub it, ship it, move on. That stopped being viable once the math changed. International films in India grew 49% in a single year (2025), their biggest jump since before the pandemic, with Hollywood’s share of the Indian box office expected to cross 20% — and the single highest-grossing international title in the entire Indian market that year was Avatar: Fire and Ash, released not in one language but stacked across Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, and the rest, because nobody serious launches a tentpole in India anymore without doing this in at least four languages simultaneously. Once a market is growing that fast and demanding that many dubbed versions just to compete, “release it in India” quietly turns into “build an India-specific release strategy,” the same way “sell payments in India” turned into UPI, and “cook lunch in India” turned into a gravity-fed kitchen.

The joke writes itself: a film can underperform in North America and still get rescued by India deciding, en masse, that it’s worth watching four times in four different dubs. Hollywood didn’t choose to care about the Indian box office out of artistic generosity. It cared because the numbers got too large to politely ignore, which is the exact same sentence you could write about UPI, cricket streaming, or roti machines, just with popcorn instead of payments.

The Connective Tissue

Here’s why this section earns its own space instead of hiding inside Food or Traffic: Akshaya Patra, the roti machine, 65 million people refreshing a cricket stream at once, a highway crew breaking Guinness records before lunch, a results website that can’t survive its own students, a voting machine invented to save a forest, and a single constituency that once out-fielded several national parliaments aren’t really seven different stories. They’re the same story as UPI’s 21.7 billion monthly transactions and the world’s least forgiving traffic junctions — something gets done so often, by so many people, that the scale itself becomes either the inventor or the breaking point, depending on who built the thing. Nobody sat down and designed a gravity kitchen, a roti robot, a streaming architecture built for 65 million concurrent refreshes, a battery-powered ballot machine, or a four-language day-and-date Hollywood release for the fun of it.

The volume demanded it — the same way 1.4 billion people doing UPI transactions demanded a fraud-detection system nobody else’s banking system ever had to build.

The same way a few lakh anxious teenagers demand, every May, that someone finally build a results page that can survive them. Different century, different industry, identical forcing function: do something casually enough times and you will eventually be forced to invent the thing nobody else needed, assuming somebody actually gets around to building it properly.

And It’s Not Just Volume — Time Runs on a Different Scale Too

In America, a 200-year-old building gets a plaque, a gift shop, and a tour guide. In India, a 200-year-old building gets a shrug and “oh, that one’s from the British time” — practically a newborn. We genuinely don’t have the same unit of “old,” because the Mundeshwari Devi Temple in Bihar, which the Archaeological Survey of India dates to around 108 CE, has had its rituals performed without interruption for over 2,000 years — not roped off behind glass as a museum exhibit, but actively prayed at this week, same as it was when the Roman Empire was still in business. We don’t measure age in centuries. We measure it in “is anyone still lighting a lamp there or not.”

And it’s not just temples. When the brochures roll out the statistics on how many international students make Stanford great, we quietly remember Nalanda. Founded in 427 CE, fully residential, completely free — tuition, food, and lodging — Nalanda housed 10,000 students and 2,000 faculty drawn from China, Korea, Japan, Tibet, and half of Central and Southeast Asia, and kept doing this for roughly 750 years before most of Europe had so much as invented the concept of a university campus. By the time it was finally destroyed in 1197, its library — reputed to hold millions of handwritten manuscripts — had been burning for months, set alight by an invading army. We didn’t just have universities first. We had the “international student body that makes a university world-class” marketing pitch a millennium and a half before anyone needed PowerPoint to make it.

Scale, it turns out, isn’t just about how many people we feed or stream cricket to. Sometimes it’s just about how far back the lamp’s still been burning, or how long ago we’d already solved the problem someone else is currently writing a press release about.

And here’s the thought that should make every AI company’s infrastructure team quietly nervous: everything in this section — the kitchens, the roti machines, the cricket streams, the highways, the box office — happened mostly in English and Hindi, with the rest of India’s languages still sitting on the sidelines, underserved by the apps that could otherwise be useful to them. If 65 million people refreshing one cricket stream can set a global concurrency record, what does that graph look like the day AI properly starts speaking Tamil, Bengali, Marathi, Telugu, and the thousand dialects underneath them — to people who’ve been waiting their whole lives for a machine that finally understands them without translation in the middle? We’ve already proven, repeatedly, that India breaks infrastructure built for “normal” scale. Nobody’s tested what happens when you remove the last barrier — language — and let thousands of millions of people in at once. That’s not a future problem. That’s the scale test nobody’s run yet.

This isn’t theoretical, either — it’s the precedent every major tech platform has already lived through. WhatsApp didn’t become the world’s defining messaging app by winning San Francisco. It became that by India alone carrying somewhere between roughly 535 million and 850-plus million users depending on whose count you trust, dwarfing every other country by a wide margin and tracking toward 900 million by the end of 2026 — more than double its nearest competitor, Brazil. Android didn’t become the dominant mobile operating system on Earth by winning America. It won because it became nearly the only operating system anyone in India could afford to put a SIM card into, at a price point and data efficiency no other market demanded as urgently. Gmail rode the same wave: cheap smartphones, thousands of millions of first-time internet users, a free inbox that didn’t choke on slow connections. None of these products are culturally Indian in any sense. But not one of them would be the global default it is today without India deciding, at scale, to adopt it first and use it constantly enough to bend the product around its own needs.

And here’s the prediction worth making out loud, given everything in this section: whichever AI India ends up adopting en masse won’t win on lab benchmarks. It’ll win because everything we’ve just walked through proves the same point over and over — scale doesn’t just stress-test a product in India, it crowns one. UPI didn’t become the world’s most-copied payments rail because it was the cleverest idea in a room. It became that because a billion-plus people hammered on it daily until whatever survived was, by definition, the best-tested system on Earth. The same forcing function is coming for AI. The model that gets adopted by India first, in India’s languages, at India’s volume, under India’s chaos, won’t just be popular in India. It’ll have already won the only stress test that actually matters globally — because nowhere else on the planet tests anything at this scale, this fast, this unforgivingly. AI companies won’t see real global returns by winning a lab benchmark. They’ll see them the day they actually win India.

The Bottomline

Every story here — UPI, fake GST invoices, Tesla, monsoons, payasam, shoes, blood pressure — has the exact same setup and almost the exact same result. Most of the time, the AIndian shows up confident, trained on the rest of the world, and gets gently, repeatedly corrected by someone who actually lives here. Once in a while — UPI, GST — it’s like a team of software developers who talk in English sometimes, Telugu in the canteen, and Hindi when their manager is around — AI has a hard time keeping up, and only the developers can understand all of it, all the time, because being born in India naturally means learning multiple languages, and using Indian financial data is a born requirement for an AIndian. Either way: it’s a tourist with excellent vision and zero local knowledge, or a local with a clipboard who still needs a senior officer to sign off. Nobody hands either of them the keys unsupervised.

Here’s where this stops being just an India story, though. Drive from Tamil Nadu to Nagaland to Kashmir and you’ve crossed three different planets of language without leaving one passport. India has been unofficially running a language bootcamp on itself for decades — 22 languages in the Constitution, hundreds more spoken, and thousands of dialects nobody’s even finished counting. That’s not a logistics headache, it’s the world’s largest unintentional practice run. So when some other country eventually asks how to build a model that actually understands its own under-documented local language — a tonal language nobody’s tokenized, a script with no keyboard, a dialect three million people speak and zero global AI labs have heard of — there’s a decent chance India already wrestled with something close enough to know exactly where the model breaks first. We’re not just going to deploy AI faster than everyone else. We’re going to be the place the rest of the world sends its language problem, because we’ve already auditioned half the planet’s grammar without leaving home.

India shouldn’t just deploy AI faster than everyone else. India should be the place the AIndian finally learns to behave — because we’re the only ones who can tell it apart from the real thing.

And maybe that’s the most honest way to end this. Like the tree of the future contained inside a seed sitting quietly in the soil, India is standing at the edge of its own sprouting. Bamboo spends its first few years doing something that looks, from the outside, like nothing at all — no visible stalk, no height to point to, nothing for an impatient onlooker to photograph. It’s down there building roots the entire time. Then, once the roots are deep enough, it shoots up faster than almost anything else on Earth grows. India has spent a few quiet decades doing exactly that underground work — UPI rails, Aadhaar, fibre networks, a billion phones, a few hundred million people learning to type, swipe, and ask a machine questions in a language it’s only just starting to understand back. None of it looked like much from outside. The visible part is just getting started. And once it actually breaks the surface, the rest of the world won’t be debating whether India caught up. It’ll just be standing there, slightly out of breath, watching us clear record after record on the way up.
