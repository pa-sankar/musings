IEX vs Others — Absolute Power Trading Overdone Absolutely?

Why IEX is an excellent contra bet today

Paa Sankar June 2026 14 min read

They say absolute power corrupts absolutely. In India's short-term electricity market, one company has held something close to absolute power for over a decade — and for the last year, the headlines have decided that power is about to end. Abruptly. Badly. Permanently.

Indian Energy Exchange has run somewhere between 85% and 95% of India's power-trading volumes for years.[1] Over the past twelve months its stock has fallen by roughly half from its peak, brokerages have queued up with "Sell" calls, and price targets have been set below where the stock already trades. The reason fits in one phrase that every financial channel has now repeated until it sounds like settled fact: market coupling.

What market coupling actually is

Today, IEX, PXIL, and HPX each run their own auction and discover their own price for the same electricity, on the same day. Market coupling pools the buy and sell orders from all three exchanges and clears them at one uniform national price. The idea, in CERC's words, is efficiency. The effect, in the market's words, is that IEX's biggest asset — being the place where the "real" price gets discovered — stops being special.

I hold IEX. Not as a trade, as a long, slow-compounding position I've been adding to. Over the last few weeks I went back through the actual paper trail behind this story — CERC's own simulations, the shadow pilot results, the exchanges' own regulatory filings, the most recent earnings calls — rather than the headline version of it. What I found doesn't match the panic.

This isn't a defence of the status quo because I happen to own it. It's a section-by-section walk through what the bashers are claiming, what the documented reality actually says, and where I land on each piece.

The market has already decided how this story ends. The filings haven't read the script.

I

The Liquidity Trial

What the Bashers Say

Once coupling arrives, electricity has one national price no matter which exchange you use. IEX's only real edge — its liquidity made its price the trustworthy one — disappears. Price stops being a differentiator, the smaller exchanges get an equal shot, and equal shot means equal share, over time.

What the Reality Is

This has actually been tested. Twice.

CERC ran its own simulations back in 2023 using real bid data across all three exchanges. Its own staff paper concluded that with one exchange holding roughly 99% of DAM and RTM volume between them, simply pooling everyone's bids together would not meaningfully improve outcomes.[2] That's the regulator's own modelling — months before the public panic even started.

Then CERC went further and ran a live shadow pilot, actually coupling exchange bids, from December 2024 to March 2025. By most independent accounts of that trial, the practical benefit was minimal.[3]

And then there's the detail almost nobody talks about, because it's buried in a regulatory filing rather than a press release. HPX — one of the two challenger exchanges — described what happened during an earlier pilot when prices across platforms were artificially aligned. By HPX's own account, within three days, trading volume on its platform had collapsed to zero. Sellers were booking losses. Buyers couldn't find anyone reliable to trade against, and simply stopped showing up.[4]

Equal price. Zero volume, inside seventy-two hours. Because nobody was reliably on the other side of the trade.

And here's a gap in the reality that I think deserves more attention than it gets: there's no clean, publicly available comparison of what IEX, PXIL, and HPX actually charge for the same megawatt at the same hour, today. Each exchange publishes its own numbers; nobody publishes the spread between them. We know the destination market coupling is meant to deliver — one uniform clearing price. We don't have a clear fix on how far apart the three starting points actually are right now. And if you only know the destination, not the starting point, the journey can still spring surprises in the short term that no amount of regulatory intent will smooth over.

Price is what you see on the screen. Liquidity is whether anyone actually shows up to trade with you. Coupling fixes the first. It does nothing for the second.

My Take

This isn't a power-market quirk — it's how every exchange works. NSE controls roughly 93% of India's cash market by turnover and close to 100% of equity futures, and BSE, which is actually the older of the two exchanges, hasn't meaningfully dented that share in decades of trying.[15] The mechanism is identical to the one HPX just admitted to: liquidity attracts liquidity, traders go where the fills already are, and simply being open for business doesn't close that gap.

A uniform price is necessary for the bear case on IEX. It is nowhere close to sufficient. The thing that took IEX over a decade to build isn't a number on a screen — it's the depth of genuine, reliable counterparties on every side of every trade, at every hour. That depth doesn't transfer the day a regulation is notified. HPX's own filing is the best evidence anyone could ask for, and it came from HPX.

II

The Integration Moat

What the Bashers Say

Even if liquidity takes time, customers will eventually drift to wherever it's cheaper or better. It's just software. Switching exchanges is no different from switching food delivery apps — a few taps, a new login, done.

What the Reality Is

On its most recent earnings call, IEX management disclosed that more than 70% of its bid volume now flows through API integration, not manual entry.[5] That number means a meaningful share of IEX's largest customers — DISCOMs, generators, large industrial buyers — have built custom software that talks directly to IEX's systems: automated bidding logic, scheduling tie-ins, internal ERP connections, all engineered around one specific exchange's API.

My Take

This is the strongest part of the stickiness argument and the one that gets the least airtime, because it isn't dramatic. Habit can be broken in a weekend. A production trading pipeline wired into your own enterprise software is not something a procurement team rips out because a rival exchange's fee is a fraction of a paisa lower. It's a re-engineering project, with operational risk attached, for an uncertain reward. Large, risk-averse counterparties do not take that trade lightly.

III

The Brokerage Myth

What the Bashers Say

Once price discovery is gone, HPX and PXIL will simply undercut IEX on transaction fees and pull volume away on cost alone.

What the Reality Is

All three exchanges currently charge transaction fees at the same regulatory ceiling — roughly two paise per unit on each side of a trade.[6] There is no brokerage war happening today. None of the three is choosing to charge less. HPX's profit margin sits near 34% against IEX's 80%+ not because HPX is cheaper, but because it is spreading a similar fixed-cost base over a fraction of the volume — pure operating leverage, nothing to do with pricing.

The one fee change that is actually coming is regulatory, not competitive: CERC has separately been weighing a cut to the fee ceiling itself, from roughly two paise to around one and a half.[6] If that happens, it compresses every exchange's per-unit economics by the same amount, IEX included. It does not hand HPX or PXIL a price advantage over IEX — it just makes the whole pie thinner for everyone equally.

My Take

This is where I think the bear case quietly conflates two different things: removing IEX's pricing edge and handing the challengers a pricing advantage. Those are not the same event. Coupling takes away IEX's shield on one front. It does not put a sword in anyone else's hand. Until CERC actually deregulates fees and a real price war starts — which is a separate, slower-moving regulatory thread — HPX and PXIL are not winning a single customer on cost. They still have to win them on something else entirely: trust that there's someone to trade with.

IV

The Shrinking Slice vs. the Growing Pie

What the Bashers Say

This is a zero-sum redistribution. Whatever IEX loses, HPX and PXIL gain, rupee for rupee.

What the Reality Is

IEX closed FY26 with its highest-ever electricity volume, up 17% year-on-year — and the segments doing the heavy lifting are mostly outside coupling's first phase entirely.[7] Real-Time Market volume grew 41% for the year and is now nearly as large as the Day-Ahead Market it's meant to be overshadowed by. Green Market volume grew over 20%. Beyond electricity, the gas exchange IGX grew volumes 28% and profit 35%; the carbon-credit platform ICX more than doubled, off a small base; a coal exchange is in the pipeline.[8]

Then there's what's happening on the grid itself. Andhra Pradesh just granted Google's Visakhapatnam data centre India's first private distribution licence outside the power sector — a category of consumer that can now procure power through bilateral deals, open access, captive generation, or exchanges, by explicit regulatory design.[9] Reliance is reportedly planning a comparable cluster nearby, backed by captive solar and battery storage.[10] These are entirely new categories of large load entering the market, with no exchange relationship to defend or poach.

And then there's the battery story, which I think is the most underrated piece of this whole picture. India's first true merchant battery storage project went commercially live trading arbitrage on IEX's own Day-Ahead Market — buying power cheap in the solar-flooded afternoon, selling it back at the evening peak. ACME Solar and Adani Green have since started doing the same.[11] This works because the price spread between midday and evening has been wide enough to cover the cost of storage since early 2024 — itself a side-effect of renewables flooding the grid.

A battery doesn't just need a good price to arbitrage. It needs a buyer reliably waiting on the other side of every single discharge window. Ask HPX how that went on day three of its own pilot.

My Take

The redistribution fight and the expansion story are running at the same time, and I think the second one is being badly underweighted in every "IEX is doomed" piece I've read. Even a shrinking percentage of a market that's compounding this fast can still be a larger absolute number five years out. And the newest, fastest-growing source of volume — merchant storage arbitrage — needs exactly the thing this whole series keeps coming back to: liquidity, not just a favourable headline price. That points toward the deepest exchange, not the newest one.

V

Can the Challengers Actually Capitalise?

What the Bashers Say

Once IEX's pricing edge is gone, HPX and PXIL naturally absorb the volume that price discovery was artificially protecting.

What the Reality Is

Look at where each of them actually stands today, not where the bull case for them assumes they'll be tomorrow.

HPX just swung back to a quarterly loss while ramping up spending on technology and people — exactly the moment it needs a war chest, it's burning cash instead.[12] Days before this piece, CERC rejected HPX's request for a three-year window to let its own anchor shareholder, PTC India — which alone accounts for roughly a third of India's bilateral power-trading volume — become an active trader on the exchange while keeping its outsized stake.[13] That was HPX's most direct shot at instantly importing real liquidity from one of the country's biggest power traders, and the regulator just closed it.

PXIL's story is older and, I think, more telling. It has existed since 2008 — the same era as IEX — and spent most of those years going nowhere. In January 2017, its own majority owners voted to shut it down for being chronically loss-making, with market share near 2% and trading volumes that were close to nil on some days. One of its board members left to join a buyout consortium, and that single departure directly hurt PXIL's renewable-certificate sales, because the volumes walked out with him.[14] Its current IPO momentum is real, but it's a very recent chapter bolted onto a very long history of irrelevance.

My Take

Removing one obstacle doesn't automatically clear the others. Price was never the only thing standing between either challenger and meaningful share — HPX's own data shows liquidity depth is a separate, unsolved problem, and PXIL's two-decade history shows execution and credibility are separate, unsolved problems too. A level playing field only matters if you're actually ready to play on it. Neither of them has shown me that yet.

It's worth being honest about the one place this analogy could cut the other way. When SEBI restricted index derivatives to a single weekly expiry, NSE's profit fell 15% in FY26 while BSE's jumped 88% — a real, recent case of a regulator's rule change redirecting volume straight to the smaller exchange.[15] So this isn't impossible. But look at how it happened: BSE already had its own liquidity pool and its own live contracts — SENSEX, BANKEX — sitting ready to absorb the redirected flow the moment the rule changed. HPX's own filing shows it doesn't have that pool to redirect into. Its problem was never that the wrong exchange got the rule change. It's that nobody reliably shows up to trade on it at all. A regulator can hand you the opportunity. It can't hand you the counterparty.

Not a Foregone Conclusion

Put the five pieces together and the picture that emerges is not the one being priced into the stock. A liquidity test that the challenger itself admits it failed. A switching cost measured in re-engineered software, not switched habits. A fee structure with no price war in it today, and a coming fee cut that hurts everyone equally rather than helping anyone selectively. A market expanding faster, in more directions, than the redistribution fight is destructive. And two challengers who, by their own numbers and their own regulatory history, haven't yet solved the problems that predate coupling and will outlast it.

None of this means market coupling is harmless, or that IEX sails through unchanged, or that I'm certain of anything here. It means the gap between "this is a real risk" and "this is a foregone collapse" is exactly where a contrarian gets paid — not by betting that coupling never happens, but by betting that its real-world bite is slower, messier, and smaller than the consensus has already priced in.

I think the strength in IEX's actual position — the liquidity, the integration, the portfolio of businesses growing around the core, the sheer size of the pie that's expanding under everyone's feet — is being badly underpriced by a market that's reacting to a regulatory headline rather than reading the regulatory filings.

I'm riding that wagon.

This is not investment advice. It's one shareholder's argument with the consensus, written down so I can hold myself to it later.
