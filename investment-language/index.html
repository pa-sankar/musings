Before You Invest a Rupee, Learn the Language

A naive investor’s notes on tax, inflation, and XIRR — and why vocabulary comes before mathematics

Paa Sankar June 2026 8 min read

Like many of you, I also started as a naive investor thinking that all I need to create wealth is to save money every month from my salary, “invest” in a good investment which gives double digit returns — avoid greedy or risky things — and voila, at the end of my working career, I will have a nice income that I can retire on. Unfortunately, today’s economics is not as simple as this, and the first thing I learnt was to understand all the different terms that the investment industry throws at you. I hope you will follow my learning journey and shorten your own to some extent. So here goes.

The first investment was never money. It was vocabulary.

There was a time when “investing” meant a choice between two people: your mother, who’d tuck it away somewhere safe and hand it back when you needed it, or the trader two streets over, who’d lend it out and return with interest and a handshake as collateral. Simple. Two options. Both people you could see, touch, and be angry at in person if something went wrong.

That world is gone. The same instinct — make my money grow, safely — now routes through an industry with hundreds of products, each one designed by someone whose job is to make their product sound like the obvious answer. Mutual funds or direct stocks. Active or passive. SIP, step-up SIP, lumpsum, STP. Thematic fund or broad category fund. Even once you’ve decided “some of this goes into equity,” you haven’t finished deciding anything — you’ve just opened a second door with twenty more doors behind it.

This isn’t a call to go enrol in a finance degree. Some of it genuinely is complicated, in places by design. But every term on that list now has a calculator sitting next to it, free, built into the same app you’d use to invest anyway. You don’t need to memorise what a word means in formula form — you need to know it exists, and what question it’s there to answer. That’s the whole bar. Investor beware starts with investor aware.

Fund selection — a season ticket to nowhere

Be passive. It works for most of us.

This is the one area where I’m still learning, so take this as a progress report, not a finished lesson.

The first thing I learnt to avoid was themes. A pharma fund, an infrastructure fund, a defence fund, an EV fund — they come around like seasons, and just like seasons, they pass. By the time you’ve noticed the theme is hot and moved your money in, you’re usually closer to the end of that season than the beginning. And then the season turns, the fund cools off, and you find yourself back roughly where you started — except now with a few years lost and a lesson paid for in real money.

Be passive. For most of us, most of the time, this is simply the right answer, and it’s the right answer precisely because it asks nothing difficult of you — no theme-timing, no manager-picking, no late-night reading of fund commentary.

I am of course referring to investing in index funds, where there is no “management” deciding which stocks to buy and how much of each — it’s all preselected by the index itself. Nifty, for example, has 50 stocks in fixed proportions, and when you hear Nifty went up 1000 points, you know exactly what that means for your investment. Just don’t panic the next day when they say Nifty crashed 800 points. Ignoring the daily headlines is a learnt art, and it’s part of the roller-coaster ride you signed up for the day you said you wanted “higher” returns. And yes — you WILL get higher returns, if you just hang on.

If you’re the kind who loses sleep over these headlines, maybe you should stick to debt funds, which invest in relatively less volatile instruments. (And if you’re wondering whether that “instrument” is a guitar or a flute — well, that’s exactly why you need to get started on learning the finance language first!) But you’ll of course have to be satisfied with lower returns. You can’t have your cake and eat it too. No risk, no reward. And no, before you ask — risk isn’t always rewarding either. Even that answer is a no, not always.

Going active asks a lot more of you than a better return. It asks you to actually know yourself first — your real risk appetite, not the one you imagine you have when markets are calm, and your ability to watch bad news scroll past your portfolio and do absolutely nothing about it. That’s a harder thing to know about yourself than any fund factsheet will ever tell you, and most of us only find out the true answer the first time we’re tested by a real fall, not a hypothetical one.

And even once you’ve answered that honestly, fund selection still isn’t what I assumed it was for the longest time — picking whoever sits at the top of the rankings table. That table moves. The fund on top this year is rarely the fund on top three years from now, because topping a chart usually means someone took a concentrated bet that happened to pay off, and concentrated bets don’t pay off forever. What I’ve noticed instead, from people who’ve actually stayed invested long enough to know, is that steady wealth tends to come from the fund sitting quietly at second, or third, or even fifth — the one that never makes headlines, but stays around that same modest rank year after year after year. Consistency, it turns out, is its own kind of skill, and it’s a far rarer one than a single great year.

That’s probably a story for another day — fund selection deserves its own piece once I’ve understood my own goals and needs a little better. For now, let’s come back to the simpler question this whole article has really been about: once you’ve put your money somewhere, what is it actually earning you?

Risk-free is not tax-free

The first crack in my “safe is safe” thinking came from my own fixed deposit. I’d see the rate quoted at the bank — 7%, sounds solid, sounds risk-free — and I genuinely believed that number was what I’d get. Nobody at the counter mentions that this is a pre-tax number, and what actually remains in your account after the tax department takes its cut is a smaller figure, sometimes meaningfully smaller if you’re in a higher slab. “Risk-free” was never lying about your principal being safe. It just never volunteered that the return wasn’t fully yours to keep.

The one thing that makes this tax so easy to forget about an FD is that you don’t pay it at the counter, the way you would with GST on a purchase. You file your tax once a year, and you’re the one who has to declare what you earned as interest and pay up — in many cases, the bank only deducts TDS if your interest crosses a certain threshold. So the deposit and the tax never sit next to each other in your mind, unless you’re the kind of person who tracks every rupee through the year. This, I think, is the first thing every FD investor has to learn, usually only after burning their fingers once — no, the return is not yours till you’ve paid the taxman his cut, at some point in the future.

I know, I know — we are not always good at maths. I certainly wasn’t, and I’m still not someone who enjoys it. But here’s the one thing I learnt the hard way: when it comes to money, doing this basic maths — three numbers, one sheet, addition and subtraction — is genuinely the line between the haves and the have-nots. Not because the have-nots can’t add. Because most of us never sit down and add it at all. So let’s actually do it, once.

I opened a blank sheet, three columns: the rate the bank promised, what I actually keep after tax, and what that’s worth once inflation takes its share too. FD rate: 7%. Inflation that year: 6%.

Note on the numbers used here

The line that stopped me cold: at the 20% and 30% slabs, my “guaranteed, risk-free” deposit was losing me money in real terms — every single year, while the passbook kept printing a bigger number. And here’s the cruel twist — the more successful you are, the higher your slab, the worse this gets. The “safe” option quietly punishes you more the more you earn.

Nobody at the bank counter is going to point this out to you. You have to open the sheet yourself.

You might be wondering what that third column is — because it seems to have even negative values. That, my friend, is the invisible thief who steals your money one day at a time. Welcome to the real world!

Don’t forget the invisible thief — Inflation

Once I understood the tax bite, I thought I’d found the real number — my honest, after-tax FD return. That’s when I ran into the second lesson, and this one stung more, because it doesn’t show up anywhere on any statement. My FD balance only ever grows. It never goes down. And yet, the year prices rose faster than my after-tax return, I was quietly poorer in terms of what that money could actually buy — even though every passbook entry showed a gain. That’s inflation, and it’s the perfect thief: it never makes an entry that looks like a loss, so most of us never go looking for it. This is also the year I started paying real attention to gold, because gold doesn’t play by rupee rules at all — more on that later.

You can’t do much about inflation — it’s controlled by things entirely outside your hands: oil prices, wars on the other side of the world, currency fluctuations, foreign investors moving money in and out. Just knowing what it means for you is already a lesson learnt, and believe me, it’s a very important one. Like everything else in maths, it adds up. Two percent one year, three percent the next, and before you know it your shirts cost 25% more, your car costs 20% more, your monthly grocery bill is up 10%. Since I was born, there has never been a single year when inflation wasn’t there — it’s always there, year after year, month after month, quietly reducing your purchasing power.

When your grandparents fondly remember buying gold at ₹35 a gram, and you check today’s price and find it’s ₹15,000 for that same gram — what do you think actually changed so much? It’s all those small numbers, adding up over decades, eating away at your rupee’s purchasing power. Here’s another way to see it: gold hasn’t moved anywhere. The rupee has lost its purchasing power. This, my friend, is the first reason gold trumps most things when it comes to wealth preservation — it’s the one thing that helps keep your savings inflation-proof.

We will talk about gold a little later — but let’s get back to our investment in mutual funds. There were still many things I had to learn before I understood why my total portfolio wasn’t moving anywhere close to what the brochures advertised. That’s when I came across the Extended Internal Rate of Return. Ah, I know — I didn’t know what that meant either, till I read up on it.

XIRR — heard of it?

For the longest time, I’d see a fund’s advertised return — say 18% CAGR — and assume that’s roughly what I was getting too, since I was invested in it. It took me embarrassingly long to realize that number describes one lump sum, invested once, left untouched for years. That was never my story. I put money in every month through SIPs, added a bit extra when I got a bonus, paused once during a rough patch. My money has its own timeline, separate from the fund’s timeline, and the fund’s CAGR has no idea any of that happened.

The word I was missing was XIRR — the honest version of “what did my money, on my exact dates, with my exact amounts, actually earn.” I never had to learn the formula. It turns out my own broker app had been quietly calculating this for me the whole time, sitting under a tab I’d never clicked. I just didn’t know to look for it, or that the number I should trust had a different name than the one in the advertisement.

Now let’s get to the numbers, to see how this actually stacks up. Say my own XIRR for the year — not the fund’s advertised CAGR, my actual money, my actual dates — worked out to a genuinely good 18%. Same exercise as before, three columns.

My equity XIRR 18.0% 15.75% ~9.2%

Sit this next to the FD table above. My FD, at best, gave me +0.9%, and at my likely slab, a small loss. My equity, after the exact same two corrections, still left me with roughly 9.2% — real money, after tax, after inflation, in my pocket. That gap between 9.2% and roughly zero (or worse) is the only number that ever mattered. Not 18%. Not 7%. The gap between what equity actually gave me and what “safe” actually gave me, both told the truth.

It took me one sheet, three columns, and maybe ten minutes to find that gap. The hardest part was never the maths. It was remembering to ask the question in the first place. And before you can even ask it, you have to learn what “today’s inflation-adjusted, risk-premium return” actually means in plain English — three intimidating words hiding one simple idea: what did I really get, after the thief took his share, compared to what I’d have gotten for taking no risk at all. You need to learn the language before you can ask the right question.

So if the only thing you take away from this entire article is that you go look up XIRR, LTCG, CAGR, and a few other acronyms you’ve been nodding along to for years without quite knowing what they mean — I’ve succeeded.

Why the calendar does more work than the rate — compounding

By now I’d stripped my “18%” down to something far more modest and far more honest — call it 8 or 9%, after tax, after inflation, after admitting it was never really 18% to begin with. I’ll be honest, my first reaction was disappointment. A single-digit number felt like a failure compared to the headline I’d started with.

What changed my mind was realizing how badly my own gut was estimating what that number would actually do over time. I’d done the lazy addition in my head — 8% a year for ten years, that’s 80%, right? It isn’t. It’s closer to 116%. Stretch it to twenty years and my mental addition said 160%; the real number was past 360%. Nobody needed to teach me a formula for this — I just needed to stop trusting my own arithmetic and let a calculator show me the gap. That gap is where wealth actually gets built in a currency that’s quietly losing ground to inflation every single year. Not by chasing a bigger rate. By giving an honest rate more years than my impatience wanted to give it.

Gold is the wealth preserver — to hand to the next generation

Compounding, I eventually understood, was solving a different problem than the one my parents’ generation worried about. They weren’t trying to grow money inside the rupee — they were trying to make sure the rupee itself didn’t betray them by the time it mattered. That’s where gold earned its place in every Indian household long before any of us had heard of a mutual fund.

A currency is a promise made by a government, and promises get diluted — slowly, through ordinary inflation, or suddenly, through a crisis. Gold has never needed anyone’s promise to hold its meaning. It wasn’t until I really sat with this that I understood why my own portfolio had drifted so heavily toward it — not as a bet on price, but as the one asset I could hand to the next generation with some confidence that it would still mean something by the time they held it.

The one mistake I had to unlearn here: the gold sitting as jewellery in a locker is not the same asset as gold sitting in a bond or a fund. I’d paid making charges and tax on jewellery for years without ever asking what happens to that money when it’s eventually sold — and the answer is, it doesn’t come back. Only the metal’s weight does. If preservation across a generation is genuinely the goal, the form the gold takes matters just as much as the gold itself.

In ancient times, people used gold — or silver, if things called for something a little cheaper — as money directly. So when someone said they were “saving money,” they meant exactly that: setting aside gold and silver coins for later use. When we switched to paper money, we kept the same idea. Put it aside for later. Save.

But there’s a big difference between gold and paper currency. No, it isn’t inflation — it’s something even more fundamental: the difference between money and currency. For simplicity, let’s say money is gold or silver. The rupee or the dollar you hold today is fiat currency — it no longer has any fixed relationship to gold, which is exactly why we keep watching gold go up in value, or you could just as easily say the rupee keeps devaluing against gold.

When paper money was first introduced, it was meant to be a stand-in for gold. The promise printed on the note was that the bank would hand you actual gold if you ever asked for it. Over time, almost nobody asked. So the promise quietly changed to say they’d give you a rupee if you asked — and what exactly is a rupee? It’s the very note sitting in your hand. If your head isn’t spinning yet, you have a steadier head than I did the first time I worked this out.

What we live with today is fiat currency — something based entirely on trust, and every fiat currency in the world is tied, one way or another, to one global fiat currency: the dollar. Before 1971, the dollar itself was anchored to gold — so a certain number of rupees meant a certain number of dollars, which in turn meant a certain weight of gold sitting in a vault somewhere. Then that last connection was severed, and just like that, we’ve continued using currency exactly as before, except now it’s floating on nothing but trust in a government. Governments can print “money” on a press — but that was never meant to work this freely. When currency was anchored to gold, printing was supposed to stay proportional to how much gold actually sat in the vault. Long story short: we’re all living in a fiat-currency world now, and it’s worth wondering, every now and then, why our ancestors bothered with gold at all.

If you want to go deeper on this, I’d point you to Mike Maloney’s Hidden Secrets of Money series on YouTube — the full playlist is here. I don’t have much more to add beyond that, except this: gold has stood as a measure of wealth for thousands of years, and I don’t expect that to change just because new technology has arrived and Bitcoin now rules the digital world. What worked for our ancestors works for me. Thank you very much — you can have all the fun you like with fiat currency and an imaginary digital wallet. I’ll stick to my gold, sitting quietly as HDFC Gold MF units.

If you are still reading this, that means you also have the same sense of needing to learn so much to ensure your hard-earned money — not fiat currency, not rupee, money — is made to work for you in your old age. I know this has been a long read, and you’re impatient to finish, but let me wrap it up in a few sentences.

The Same Lesson, Every Time

Every section above taught me a version of the same thing: the number on the screen is never the whole story, and the whole story usually has a name I hadn’t bothered to learn yet. Risk-free isn’t tax-free. A growing balance isn’t a growing fortune if inflation is outrunning it. A fund’s CAGR isn’t your XIRR. An 18% headline isn’t an 18% reality. Even gold and jewellery, which look like the same metal, aren’t the same investment.

None of this required me to become good at mathematics. It required me to stop assuming I already understood a word just because I’d heard it often enough.

I went looking for a good investment. I came back instead with a good vocabulary — the investment followed on its own.
